Module 2 · Reading the room
Social awareness in practice
Emotional intelligence isn't just about understanding yourself — it's about reading others accurately. Social awareness means picking up on emotional cues, understanding group dynamics, and noticing what's not being said.
KEY CONCEPT
People with high social awareness notice the gap between what someone says and what their body language, tone, or energy communicates. That gap is where the real information lives.
SCENARIO — What would you do?

You're in a team sync. Your colleague Ana presents her project update. She says everything is "on track" and "going well" — but she's speaking faster than usual, avoiding eye contact, and didn't mention the deadline that was due last week. Your manager moves on without asking any questions.

What's the most emotionally intelligent response?
✓ Well read. You noticed the gap between Ana's words and her signals — and you chose a response that's supportive without putting her on the spot. That's social awareness in action. Checking in privately creates psychological safety and gives Ana the chance to be honest.
Not quite. High social awareness means acting on what you noticed — not dismissing it or escalating before you've talked to the person directly. The most effective move is almost always a private, low-pressure check-in first.

Check-in rounds
A structured opening that gets everyone present before diving into business.
5 minAny sizeNo prep

Purpose

Help everyone arrive mentally, not just physically. Creates safety and connection before the work starts.

How to run it

1

Open with one question for the whole team. Example: "In one word, how are you showing up today?"

2

Go around the room — each person answers in 10-15 seconds. No elaboration needed.

3

Facilitator (you) goes last. Then move into the agenda.

Good questions to use

"One word for how you're feeling right now." · "What's one thing on your mind today?" · "Weather report: what's your internal forecast?"

Retro in a box
A 30-minute guided retrospective that works for any team, any project, any size.
30 minAny sizePost-sprint

Purpose

Create space to reflect on what's working and what isn't — before moving into the next cycle. Builds team learning habits.

How to run it

1

Set the stage (3 min): Remind the team this is a safe space — no blame, just learning.

2

Gather data (10 min): Everyone writes sticky notes for: What went well? What was hard? What surprised us?

3

Group and discuss (10 min): Cluster similar themes. Pick 2-3 to discuss as a team.

4

Decide (7 min): Pick one thing to try differently next time. Assign an owner.

Working styles
Help your team understand how each person works best — and design around it.
20 min4-12 peopleNew teams

Purpose

Reduce friction before it happens. When people know each other's preferences, they collaborate better and assume less.

How to run it

1

Each person answers 3 questions in writing (5 min): How do you like to receive feedback? What drains your energy at work? What does support look like to you?

2

Each person shares one answer — the one they want the team to know most. (10 min)

3

Close with: "What's one thing you'll do differently based on what you heard?" (5 min)

Team compass
Align on priorities and ways of working at the start of a new cycle or quarter.
45 minAny sizeCycle kick-off

Purpose

Get everyone pointing in the same direction before the sprint starts — not halfway through it.

How to run it

1

North star (10 min): What is the one thing this team must achieve this cycle? Write it together.

2

Current reality (10 min): What's in our way? What do we have going for us?

3

Ways of working (15 min): What are our 3 agreements for how we'll work together this cycle?

4

Commit (10 min): Each person names one personal commitment to the team for this cycle.

Ideation sprint
A structured 25-minute creative session for any challenge or decision your team is facing.
25 min4-8 peopleProblem-solving

Purpose

Generate ideas fast without the usual meeting dynamics — where the loudest voice wins.

How to run it

1

Frame the challenge (3 min): Write a "How might we…" question on the board. Everyone agrees on the framing.

2

Silent generation (7 min): Everyone writes ideas individually — no talking. Quantity over quality.

3

Share and cluster (10 min): Each person shares their top 2 ideas. Group similar ones.

4

Dot vote (5 min): Each person gets 3 votes. Most votes = the idea to develop further.

Appreciation circle
A simple, low-pressure way to close a sprint, a tough week, or a big milestone.
10 minAny sizeClosing ritual

Purpose

End on connection, not just output. Recognizing each other's contributions takes 10 minutes and costs nothing — but it changes how people feel about coming back tomorrow.

How to run it

1

Go around the team. Each person names one colleague and says specifically what they appreciated about them this week or sprint.

2

Keep it specific — "thanks for your help" doesn't land. "Thanks for staying on the call when it ran over and helping me think through the decision" does.

3

No one responds to their appreciation — just receives it. Facilitator closes with a word of thanks to the whole team.
